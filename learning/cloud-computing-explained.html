Cloud Computing Explained

You've probably heard the phrase "the cloud", but what does it actually mean? Despite the name, your files aren't floating around in the sky. Cloud computing is simply a way of storing data and running software on computers located somewhere else instead of on your own device.

What is Cloud Computing?

Cloud computing means using computing services—such as storage, software, and processing power—over the internet instead of relying only on your own computer.

Instead of buying a powerful computer or maintaining your own server, you use someone else's infrastructure whenever you need it.

Those computers live inside highly secure data centres located around the world.

A Simple Analogy

Think of electricity.

Most people don't own a power plant. They simply plug into the electrical grid whenever they need power.

Cloud computing works much the same way.

Instead of owning expensive servers, businesses rent computing power only when they need it.

Cloud vs. Local Storage

Local Storage

  • Files are stored on your computer.
  • If your hard drive fails, you could lose your data.
  • You can usually only access the files from that device.

Cloud Storage

  • Files are stored securely on remote servers.
  • Your data is backed up across multiple systems.
  • You can access your files from almost any device with an internet connection.

Examples You Probably Use Every Day

Service How it Uses the Cloud
Microsoft 365 Stores documents, email, Teams chats, and calendars online.
Google Drive Stores your files so they're available anywhere.
Dropbox Synchronizes files across multiple devices.
Netflix Streams movies from cloud servers instead of downloading everything first.
Spotify Streams music from cloud infrastructure.
iCloud Backs up your photos, contacts, and device settings.

Why Businesses Love the Cloud

  • No expensive servers to purchase.
  • Employees can work from anywhere.
  • Automatic backups reduce the risk of data loss.
  • Resources can grow as the business grows.
  • Software updates happen automatically.
  • Teams can collaborate in real time.

Is the Cloud Safe?

A common misconception is that storing files in the cloud is automatically unsafe.

In reality, major cloud providers invest billions of dollars each year into security, redundancy, encryption, and monitoring—far more than most small businesses could afford on their own.

However, cloud security is a shared responsibility.

Using strong passwords, enabling multi-factor authentication (MFA), and being cautious of phishing attacks remain essential.

Where Are My Files Actually Stored?

Your files aren't stored "in the cloud" as a magical place.

They're stored on physical servers inside secure data centres owned by companies like Microsoft, Google, Amazon, or Apple.

The cloud simply gives you convenient access to those servers over the internet.

Cloud Computing in Everyday Life

Every day, millions of people use cloud computing without realizing it.

  • Checking Gmail
  • Watching YouTube
  • Joining a Microsoft Teams meeting
  • Sharing files in OneDrive
  • Backing up photos from your phone
  • Playing online games
  • Shopping online

Cloud computing has become one of the foundations of modern technology.

Key Takeaways

  • Cloud computing means using computers and storage over the internet instead of on your own device.
  • Your files are stored on secure servers inside professional data centres.
  • Services like Microsoft 365, Google Drive, Netflix, and Dropbox all rely on cloud computing.
  • The cloud makes it easier to access files, collaborate with others, and keep data backed up.
  • Cloud services are secure, but users still need good security habits like strong passwords and MFA.
